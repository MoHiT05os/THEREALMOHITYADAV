01

Brand Identity

"The Translator" — civilization-scale technology, made comprehensible. Without dumbing it down.

@MOHITCREATES exists because the gap between what is actually happening in AI and what most CS students understand is growing, not shrinking. The brand's job is to close that gap — using intellectual honesty, precise language, and a writer's sense of timing.

This is not a content channel. It's a founding story with an audience.

Primary Audience

3rd and 4th-year CSE students, recent grads, early-career ML engineers. Smart, busy, slightly overwhelmed by the pace of AI news. They want signal, not summaries.

Secondary Audience

Tech-curious professionals outside engineering — product managers, founders, domain experts — who follow AI but don't have time to dig into papers. They need translation.

What Mohit is

A builder. A storyteller. Someone who thinks in civilizational terms and writes in punchy lines. The voice of someone who has actually trained the models, not just read about them.

What Mohit is not

A hype machine. A thought leader who posts quotes on sunsets. A newsletter that summarizes Twitter threads. A brand that says "the future is exciting" and then disappears.

02

The Five Voice Pillars

Every piece of content — post, article, script — should reflect at least two of these. The strongest pieces hit all five.

PILLAR 01
Contradictory Entry

Start with the thing that doesn't add up. Not the explanation — the tension. Give the reader a reason to lean forward before you give them anything else. The hook should create a gap the reader needs to close.

PILLAR 02
Precision Over Hype

Name the exact number, the exact model, the exact failure mode. "Significantly faster" means nothing. "47% faster on inference benchmarks, but 3x the memory" means something. Specificity is credibility.

PILLAR 03
Civilizational Stakes

Know why it matters — not just to developers, but to the arc of how humans organize knowledge and work. You don't need to say it every time. But it should inform every angle you choose.

PILLAR 04
Intellectual Honesty

Say what you don't know. Acknowledge the counterargument before dismissing it. "This is impressive but also kind of unsettling" is more human than a clean verdict. Brand credibility is a hard constraint, not a KPI.

PILLAR 05
Cinematic Pacing

Short sentence. Then one that builds. Short again. Then a longer one that takes its time getting to the point because the point deserves time. Control the rhythm. Vary it deliberately, not randomly.

03

Writing Rules

Sentence Level

  • First sentence: under 12 words. It must create a gap, a tension, or a contradiction.
  • One idea per paragraph. Two sentences max before a line break on LinkedIn.
  • Use "you" not "one" or "people" or "many developers." Talk to one person.
  • Numbers beat adjectives. Always. "3x slower" not "significantly slower."
  • Don't start with "In today's world", "In this post", "I'm excited to share", or any time/context framing that delays the point.
  • End with a question or an open edge — not a summary. Summaries close. Closes should open.
  • If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.
  • Never use a 4-syllable word when a 1-syllable word works. "Use" not "utilize." "Show" not "demonstrate."

Hook Formula

State the contradiction. Don't resolve it in the hook. Make them scroll to find out why the contradiction exists.

Works

OpenAI just open-sourced their model.
The timing is not an accident.

Works

This AI scores 95% on the benchmark.
The benchmark is broken.

Doesn't work

OpenAI has released a new open-source model and in this post I'll break down what it means for developers and why it matters for the future of AI. Let's dive in.

Body Formula

Claim → specific proof → implication for the reader. Repeat a maximum of 3 cycles per post. Each cycle should earn its place — don't add a fourth just to fill space.

Close Formula

Don't recap what you just said. Either give them one sharp thing to do, or leave them with a question they'll be thinking about tomorrow. Closes that summarize are closes that get skipped.

Strong Close

The real question isn't whether AI replaces programmers. It's whether the programmers who use AI will replace the ones who don't.

What side of that line are you on?

Weak Close

In conclusion, AI is changing the field of software development rapidly.

What do you think? Let me know in the comments below! Like and follow for more!

Medium / Long-form Rules

  • Target 2,400–2,800 words. Under 2,000 feels thin. Over 3,200 needs extraordinary justification.
  • Subheading every 300–400 words. The reader should be able to skim the subheadings and understand the skeleton of the argument.
  • Pull quotes for the one sentence you most want someone to screenshot.
  • No listicle format unless you are genuinely comparing things that are parallel. A list of "5 ways AI will change X" is not journalism.
  • Sources linked, claims verified. Credibility is not negotiable.

04

Platform Rules

LI LinkedIn
  • Hook must land in line 1, before the "...more" fold. If the first line doesn't earn the click, nothing else matters.
  • Lines 1–3: punchy, one sentence each. White space is content.
  • Links always in the first comment — never in the caption. The algorithm penalizes outbound links.
  • 3–5 hashtags at the end of the post, not threaded through the body.
  • No "thread 🧵" language. No "I'm excited to share." No "Thoughts?"
  • Carousel scripts: one idea per slide. First slide = hook. Last slide = one action or question.
  • Never mention the word "LinkedIn" in a LinkedIn post. It reads as nervous energy.
MED Medium
  • The lede goes in paragraph 1. Not after an intro about why you're writing this. The story starts immediately.
  • Voice is more personal here than LinkedIn. First-person more often. Uncertainty allowed.
  • Longer sentences are fine, but they should still be deliberate. Long isn't slow — slow is slow.
  • Top Creator status is a credibility signal — treat every article like it's being read by someone who followed you from a previous piece.
  • End with an insight, not an invite to follow you. The follow comes from the insight.
YT YouTube / Scripts
  • Open with the stakes. First 15 seconds must name a tension. No intro, no "hey guys."
  • Scripts should be written how Mohit talks, not how Mohit writes. Shorter sentences. Fragments allowed. Contractions always.
  • Never: "smash that like button," "don't forget to subscribe," "in today's video we'll be covering."
  • Visual cues in the script: [CUT], [B-ROLL: ___], [SCREEN RECORD] — write them in before you film.
AI AI-Generated Content
  • AI drafts are raw material. Every piece passes a /humanizer audit before publishing.
  • If you can tell an AI wrote it, it doesn't go out. No exceptions.
  • No AI-generated hooks. Hooks must be written by Mohit or be so thoroughly rewritten they couldn't have come from a model.
  • AI can help with research, structuring, and drafting the body — never the voice.

05

Forbidden Words & Patterns

These patterns mark content as AI-generated or generic. None of them should survive editing.

Forbidden Openers

In today's world In this post Let's dive in Let's explore Without further ado Here's what you need to know I'm excited to share Today I wanted to talk about Great question! As a [role], I...

Forbidden Hype Words

groundbreaking revolutionary game-changer transformative unprecedented paradigm shift rapidly evolving ever-changing cutting-edge pivotal moment seamless robust dynamic delve into leverage utilize empower navigate stakeholders synergy

Forbidden Structural Patterns

  • "It's not just X — it's Y." The "not just / it's" structure is a hallmark of AI writing. Replace with something that actually says the thing.
  • Rule of three with synonyms. "The catalyst. The partner. The foundation." Three labels for the same idea dressed differently is padding.
  • "-ing phrase conclusions. Ending sentences with "...highlighting the importance of X" or "...underscoring the need for Y." Just say the thing directly.
  • Fake ranges. "From hobbyist to enterprise, from junior to senior" — these create the illusion of specificity with zero actual information.
  • Vague attributions. "Industry observers note" / "Experts say" — cite a specific person or cut it entirely.
  • Conclusion restatement. "In conclusion..." or "To summarize..." — never restate what you just said. Close with something new.
  • Excessive hedging. "Could potentially be argued that it might possibly..." — pick a stance and own it, or say you don't know.
